# VC Pitch Deck Frameworks Research

## 1. Sequoia Capital (12 slides, exact order)

Title → Company Purpose → Problem → Solution → **Why Now** → Market Size → Competition → Product → Business Model → Team → Financials → Next Steps / Ask

Key emphasis:
- **Company Purpose** = single declarative sentence (not a mission statement)
- **Why Now** is Sequoia's most distinctive slide — forces you to argue *timing* via historical category evolution + recent enabling trends
- Investors spend seconds on decks; every word must earn its place

## 2. First Round Capital

No official template. Key patterns from their Review blog:

- **Burning Questions Exercise**: list 10-15 investor questions before writing a slide
- Open with what you do, traction before the midpoint
- Address your #1 objection in the first 10 minutes ("steer into the concern")
- Team slide goes *late*, not early — team only registers after investors grasp the problem's scale
- **"Altitude shifting"** — best pitches zoom between vision and operational detail on demand

Their "bullshit meter" flags:
- Quasi-team members
- Pipeline inflation
- Vanity metrics
- TAM games
- No deployment plan for capital

## 3. Peter Thiel / Founders Fund

Not a slide framework — a set of *filters*:

- **Contrarian truth test**: "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" Your pitch must have this at its center.
- **10x test**: Product must be 10x better on at least one measurable dimension. "Better UX" fails.
- **Monopoly framing**: Start in a tiny market you can dominate → concentric expansion circles → identify your actual moat (technology, network effects, scale, or brand)
- **DO NOT pitch a huge competitive market** — that signals commodity

## 4. Reid Hoffman / LinkedIn Series B (26 annotations)

Key principles from the actual annotated deck:

- Open with 3-8 bullet **investment thesis**; everything else is evidence for those
- **Concept pitch vs. data pitch** — know which mode you're in; don't fake traction
- Pitch by **analogy to businesses investors already know** are valuable
- Eliminate all hedging qualifiers ("very," "quite") — they flag nervousness
- One primary revenue stream only
- **Begin AND end with the investment thesis** (thesis slide stays up during Q&A)
- Appendix slides for anticipated objections

## 5. Guy Kawasaki's 10/20/30 Rule

**10 slides:** Title → Problem/Opportunity → Value Proposition → Underlying Magic → Business Model → Go-to-Market Plan → Competitive Analysis → Management Team → Financial Projections → Current Status + Ask

**20 minutes** (setup takes 10, Q&A needs 30 of the 60-minute meeting)

**30pt minimum font** — forces ruthless cutting

Update: Decks now need to be **email-readable without narration** since VCs often read async before meetings.

## 6. Techstars vs 500 Global

| | YC | Techstars | 500 Global |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary filter | Idea + traction velocity | Team + mentor fit | Metrics + market size |
| Demo Day | ~2 min | ~6 min | Variable |
| What they add | Network + brand | Mentorship intensity | Growth rigor |

**Techstars:** Team-first filter. 6-minute Demo Day pitch. Skip padding advisor slides.

**500 Global / Dave McClure:** AARRR metrics lens on everything. The "money shot" product demo goes early. Markets must be $1B+. "Baby language" clarity test.

## 7. The "Why Now?" Slide

Five forces that create timing arguments:
1. **Technology shifts** — new capability that didn't exist before
2. **Market evolution** — behavior change that creates openings
3. **Regulatory changes** — new rules that create/destroy markets
4. **Economic forces** — cost shifts that change what's viable
5. **Incumbent blind spots** — what big players can't or won't do

The timing test:
- Could this work 5 years ago? → If yes, "why now" is weak
- Will it work in 5 years? → If yes for anyone, what's your head start?
- What concrete evidence proves the shift?
- Why can't incumbents adapt?

**Strongest:** Convergence of multiple forces simultaneously
**Weakest:** Single trend that applies to every business

## 8. Universal Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| "We're targeting 1% of a $4T market" | You haven't defined your customer |
| Magic quadrant (you top-right, everyone else bottom-left) | Signals you can only differentiate on two axes |
| "We have no competition" | No research or no market demand |
| Hockey stick without mechanism | Investors know it's Excel; show the driver |
| 5-year precision projections | Nobody believes them; 18 months is credible |
| "First mover advantage" | Not a real moat. Google wasn't first. |
| Long advisor lists | Investors back founders, not advisory boards |
| "Better UX" as moat | Can be copied; not venture-scale |
| AI-washing (2025+) | "AI-powered" without specifying what the AI does |
| Missing the ask | Pitch ends without stating amount + terms + milestones |
