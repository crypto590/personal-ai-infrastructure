# Sports-Tech Pitch Research

## 1. Successful Sports-Tech Raises — What They Did

### Strava — $110M Series F (TCV + Sequoia, Nov 2020)
- Started with cyclists only, added sports later
- Social layer on GPS data created community lock-in before monetization
- Pitch led with: 2M new users/month, 70M members, 20% YoY revenue growth
- Now: $265M ARR, 90% from subscriptions, ~$2.2B valuation, 180M users
- Engagement: 2.23% per post — 15x Facebook, 45x Twitter

### Whoop — $404.8M total, $3.6B valuation (Series F)
- Original thesis: "People lack quantifiable, physiological data about their bodies"
- Key pivot (2018/2019): killed $500 hardware price → Device-as-a-Service ($18-30/month, device free)
- That pivot unlocked investment: SaaS-style metrics on a hardware company
- Official wearable for PGA, NFL, CrossFit, WTA before pitching later rounds
- Athlete-investors (equity + ambassador): Mahomes, Phelps, McIlroy, Ronaldo
- Metrics: 93% enterprise renewal rate; >50% daily engagement 18+ months post-purchase

### Hudl — $230M total, $72.5M Series D (Accel)
- Pain: coaches spent 4-6 hours manually cutting film → Hudl collapsed to <1 hour
- Started with high school football in Nebraska only
- Switching costs: coaches build entire game plans around the platform
- Institutional adoption: school buys → every coach, athlete, staff uses it

### TeamSnap — 25M users, ~$58M ARR, 26% growth
- Compulsory network effect: league adopts → all teams must use → all parents must use
- No viral growth needed — organizational mandate drives adoption

### Catapult Sports — $126M revenue, $846M market cap
- Born from Australian Institute of Sport research (academic credibility as launch pad)
- Proved at Olympic/elite level, democratized downward
- 5,000+ elite teams in 100+ countries
- Price inelasticity: teams don't swap analytics providers mid-season

### Pattern Across All Successful Pitches
1. Named a precise user (not "athletes" — "endurance coaches" or "high school football coaches")
2. Embedded into a daily professional workflow
3. Created compulsory or structural adoption mechanisms
4. Quantified outcomes, not features: "41 more minutes of sleep per night" (Whoop/Harvard)
5. Secured institutional partnerships before pitching — not after

## 2. TAM Framing for Sports-Tech

**The winning framework:**

1. **Bottom-up only.** (addressable customers) × (your price). No Gartner citations without your own math
2. **Wedge → category rights.** Show how owning a narrow segment gives you the right to expand
3. **Segment credibly.** TAM (all athlete software) → SAM (amateur endurance, North America) → SOM (realistic 3-year capture)
4. **Cite institutional capital flow as market validation.** $2.31B in H1 2025 in sports-tech VC
5. **Use comparable exits for return math.** 4-12x revenue multiples at exit

**Context numbers (frame, don't use as your TAM):**
- Global sports tech market: $29.74B (2024) → $68-91B by 2030
- Youth sports: $100B+ market, 10.68% annual growth
- Sports-tech VC: $2.31B across 58 rounds in H1 2025

## 3. Overcoming "Vitamin vs. Painkiller"

**Argument 1: Injury prevention = financial liability.**
Injury to a competitive athlete is financially catastrophic (scholarship, contract, ranking). Prevention is liability management.

**Argument 2: Performance data = competitive survival.**
When peers are tracked and you're not, being data-dark is the pain. At the level of scholarships and contracts, this is not optional.

**Argument 3: The real customer may not be the athlete.**
Consumer sports apps are vitamins. When a coach/team/league uses the data to make decisions, it's a painkiller for the institution.

**Argument 4: Switching costs create lock-in.**
Months of training history, performance trends, PR records don't migrate. Vitamins have zero switching cost. Painkillers accumulate it.

**Argument 5: Lead with engagement, not features.**
Whoop's >50% daily engagement 18+ months post-purchase. Athletes don't check vitamins every morning.

## 4. Network Effects in Sports Platforms

**Type 1: Compulsory/Institutional (strongest)**
Organization deploys → every member uses by definition.
- TeamSnap: league → teams → parents
- Hudl: school → coaches → athletes
- Pitch: "Our growth is structural, not viral."

**Type 2: Social/Community (Strava model)**
Athletes follow athletes. Segments become competitive. Clubs form.
- Strava: 14B kudos in 2025; engagement 15x Facebook
- Pitch: "Every new user makes the platform more valuable for every existing user."

**Type 3: Data Network Effects (Catapult/Whoop)**
More athletes → better benchmarks → better AI → more valuable to new athletes.
- Catapult: 5,000+ teams, 40+ sports — dataset can't be replicated quickly
- Pitch: "Data ownership is the new network effect."

## 5. Athlete Endorsement Strategy

**The Whoop playbook (three stages):**
1. Organic elite adoption — authentic proof, no payment
2. Official institutional partnerships — PGA, NFL, CrossFit, WTA
3. Athlete-investor hybrid — equity stake + ambassador

**Social proof ranked by investor impact (early stage):**
1. Usage data from named athletes with quantified outcomes
2. Outcome-specific testimonials from coaches/ADs
3. Advisory or equity involvement from credible athlete/coach

**For early-stage:** One athletic director who has embedded your product into their workflow > any celebrity tweet.

## 6. Revenue Models — VC Preference

1. **B2B SaaS institutional contracts** (most fundable) — high switching costs, predictable ARR
2. **B2B/B2C hybrid subscription** — freemium top-of-funnel, premium drives revenue
3. **Hardware + subscription / DaaS** — hardware is acquisition cost, subscription is the business
4. **Marketplace** — works when both sides underserved; must show which side you seed first
5. **Advertising/media** (weakest early-stage) — requires massive scale

## 7. Common Sports-Tech Pitch Failures

1. **Fan-facing product, no monetization path** — "fans want this" is not a business
2. **Top-down TAM** — "$70B sports tech market" includes broadcast infrastructure and gambling
3. **High CAC, low LTV consumer app** — without institutional channels, CAC kills unit economics
4. **Vitamin framing** — "we help athletes track performance" is a feature, not a pain
5. **No institutional validator** — zero coaches/ADs willing to put their name on it
6. **Wrong investor target** — generalist VCs won't do sports-tech. Target: Courtside Ventures, Elysian Park, Arctos, Comcast SportsTech, Bolt
7. **No B2B unlock** — consumer → data/distribution → B2B monetization is the model that works
8. **Hardware without subscription** — gets penalized on multiples
