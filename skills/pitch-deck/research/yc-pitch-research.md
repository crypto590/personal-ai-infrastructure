# YC Pitch Research

## Michael Seibel's Seven Questions Framework

**1. What do you do?**
- Keep it simple: "You want to explain what you do in the simplest language possible."
- Use the **user path method** if struggling: Walk through how customers actually interact with your product rather than explaining the mechanics.
- Example: "We're Google. We build a website with a box in it. You type any question into that box and we show you websites that answer that question."
- Goal: Generate follow-up questions, not complete understanding
- "Make it sound dumber than you think it should."

**2. How big is the market?**
- Bottom-up over top-down: (addressable customers) × (price)
- Research existing markets or estimate potential customers × price
- Avoid assuming too broad a customer base

**3. What's your progress?**
- Demonstrate work output relative to time invested
- **Always include timeframe** — "500 users" means nothing without "in 3 weeks"
- Prioritize: product development and customers over fundraising/biz dev
- "I'm much more interested in progress than genius ideas."

**4. What's your unique insight?**
- Go beyond the problem statement — what you know that others don't
- Source: customer conversations, competitive analysis, personal experience
- Must be specific: "email inbox functions as a personal database users never want to delete from" (Gmail)
- NOT: "email is broken"

**5. What's your business model?**
- Own a simple, clear model. Pick one and commit.
- Common mistake: listing 5 different monetization ideas
- "We will charge for premium features" is too vague

**6. Who's on your team?**
- Number of founders, technical co-founder presence
- Relationship length, full-time commitment, equity split
- Relevant expertise only (ignore GPA, generic corporate experience)

**7. What do you want?**
- Make a direct ask. "If you want me to invest, ask."
- Common mistake: ending without asking for money

## The One-Liner Test (Email Test)

Write a two-sentence pitch, email to a smart friend, ask them to explain it back. If they ask clarifying questions, revise. This prevents you from adding explanations mid-conversation.

Eliminate: jargon, acronyms, marketing speak, ambiguous terms like "platform."

## YC Demo Day Format

- ~2 minutes per company
- Lead with whatever is most impressive about your company
- Don't follow a standard template order — lead with your strongest point
- No traction slide is better than a bad traction slide

## Common YC Pitch Mistakes

1. **Vague answers** — most common rejection reason
2. **No timeframe on traction** — "500 users" vs "500 users in 3 weeks since launch"
3. **"We have no competitors"** — red flag: either no research or no market
4. **Talking about who you'll hire on the ask slide** — investors want milestones, not org charts
5. **Treating the pitch like a book report** — should draw investors into conversation
6. **Not talking to customers** — if you've done rigorous discovery, this differentiates you
7. **Passion-driven pitching of weak ideas** — clarity trumps passion
